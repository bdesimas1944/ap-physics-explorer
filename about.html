The project

About this site

This is a free, open AP Physics 1 learning environment. Twenty-four interactive simulations covering Units 1 through 4 of the College Board curriculum, with Units 5 through 8 in development. A local four-state progress tracker. No login, no account, no ads, no server. Built in plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript because the learning is the point, not the platform.

It started as a focused tool for teaching AP Physics 1. The aim was to build something available in real-time — for the teacher and students to engage with during the lecture, go home and keep exploring, and learn more, and deeper, than ever before. I see this model as the way such a complex topic should be taught — from first principles, not from coverage. So I built simulations that forced the concepts to reveal themselves, rather than explaining them. Explaining, defining, and naming would follow. This is the equivalent of how children experience their first physics lessons with Hot Wheels and loop-the-loop tracks, or compete by clamping a lifted track to launch a car the greatest distance, or land on a continuation track. These were the games we played as kids — learning by doing, by playing, making up rules, with no parents or teachers to prevent discovery. Most importantly, we had fun.

I soon discovered I was building something else: a demonstration of a theory of learning I had been developing for years. The simulations are not just physics tools. They are environments designed around a specific set of ideas about how human beings acquire durable understanding — ideas I had been testing on myself, on students, and on soccer players as the Architect of Discovery. I now realize the best way to promote these ideas at scale is to build the ecosystem for many such Architects, and to provide a path for more Architects to emerge naturally. This is the future of education — a bottom-up revolution that is necessary and long overdue.

The book
Published 2026
Learning With AI
A Guide to Mastery, Curiosity, and the Art of Teaching Yourself

Every design decision on this site traces back to an argument made in this book. The simulations are built the way they are because of what Carroll proved in 1963 and Bloom proved in 1968. The progress tracker is self-reported rather than gated because of what the research says about intrinsic versus extrinsic motivation. The mastery layer is being held back because of what the wrong version of AI tutoring does to a learner's ability to struggle productively.

The book makes the case from first principles — through case studies, cognitive science, and forty years of learning in many different domains — that human beings have always known how to learn, that institutions broke the process, and that AI, used correctly, can restore it. physicsexplorer.com is what that looks like when it runs.
